# TORNADO ALLEY v2 — Storm Chasers' Showdown

## What Went Wrong With v1

The original Tornado Alley (5.9/10) had a killer theme stapled to a broken mechanic. DIY bucket vortex cannons that *might* not move a whiffle ball. Invisible air you can't see or feel. 3-4 seconds of reload downtime per shot. A concept that photographed as "kids hunched over buckets." The town was set dressing on a gamble.

**v2 keeps the theme and rebuilds everything else.**

## The Pitch

Two players stand at opposite ends of a miniature Main Street — a flat tabletop town with buildings, fences, trees, and six storm shelters. Each player wields a corded handheld blower on a tethered boom arm, aiming it down through an open-top arena. Lightweight balls (ping pong balls, not whiffle balls) sit scattered across Main Street. You're the tornado. Blast your balls into your color-coded storm shelters before time runs out.

The town fights back. Buildings block your wind. Ribbon streamers on every rooftop show you exactly where your air is going. Pinwheels on fence posts spin up when your blast hits them. Lightweight "debris" (confetti, foam leaves, tissue paper scraps) swirl and scatter across the table, making every gust visible. When a ball finally drops into a shelter, the one-way flap CLACKS shut and a little flag pops up.

Your opponent is doing the same thing from the other end. Their wind crosses yours. Balls collide. Debris flies both directions. The town is caught in the crossfire of two competing storm systems.

**This isn't air plinko. This is a tabletop disaster movie you control with wind.**

## Why It Works Now (v1 Fixes)

| v1 Problem | v2 Solution |
|---|---|
| Bucket vortex cannons might not move balls | Corded handheld blowers — proven, continuous, reliable airflow |
| Invisible air (can't see the vortex) | Ribbon streamers, pinwheels, confetti debris, optional fog — wind is VISIBLE everywhere |
| 3-4 sec reload downtime per shot | Continuous blower = continuous action. No reload. Trigger on, wind flows |
| 3D printing dependency for all buildings | Scrap wood blocks + paint. Only shelter flaps and pinwheel mounts are printed |
| Whiffle balls too heavy for air | Ping pong balls (2.7g vs 24g) — 9x lighter, blown easily at 2+ feet |
| Acrylic lid adds glare, cost, failure point | Open top. Boom-arm tethers keep blowers aimed downward into the arena. No lid needed |
| Kids can't physically operate the mechanic | Squeeze trigger on a 1.5 lb blower. A kindergartner can do it |

## The Scene

**Setting:** Small-town Main Street, mid-tornado. Everything is mid-chaos.

The tabletop is painted matte black with white/yellow road lines — Main Street running lengthwise, cross streets at intervals. Buildings are painted wood blocks and cylinders: a red barn, a white church with a steeple (dowel + triangle cap), a silver water tower (soup can on dowel legs), a row of colorful houses, and picket fences made from paint stir sticks.

Every building has **ribbon streamers** — 8-inch strips of surveyor's flagging tape tacked to rooftops. At rest, they droop. When wind hits, they snap horizontal and flutter wildly, showing airflow direction and strength in real time. The visual effect: a town caught in a storm, with dozens of ribbons whipping in different directions as two players blast from opposite ends.

**Fence-post pinwheels** (metallic Mylar party pinwheels, $0.50 each) are mounted on 12 fence posts around the field. They spin up when wind passes over them, creating flashes of reflected light. From 20 feet away, you see a tabletop full of spinning, flashing, fluttering motion. It looks alive.

**Debris field:** Before each round, the operator scatters a handful of confetti, small foam leaves, and tissue paper scraps across Main Street. These have zero gameplay function — they're pure spectacle. When both blowers fire, debris swirls, lifts, and scatters. Some of it blows off the table entirely. The visual: a tiny town being ripped apart by competing wind storms. Parents are already filming.

## The Blower Mechanics

### Hardware: Corded Craft Blowers

Not M18 Milwaukee blowers (too powerful at 120 MPH for a tabletop game with ping pong balls). Instead: **small corded craft/inflator blowers** — the kind used for bounce houses or air mattresses, modified with a nozzle reducer.

| Spec | Value |
|---|---|
| Blower type | 450W bounce house inflator ($25-30 each) |
| Output | ~2.0 PSI, adjustable via inline damper valve |
| Nozzle | Reduced from 4" to 1.5" outlet via 3D-printed cone adapter |
| Weight at nozzle | ~1.5 lbs (blower body stays on the ground, only the hose + nozzle is held) |
| Reach | 6ft flex hose from blower to nozzle — full table coverage |

### The Boom Arm System

Each player's blower nozzle is attached to a **tethered boom arm** — a lightweight PVC pipe pivoting from a vertical post at the player's end of the table. The boom arm constrains the nozzle to aim downward into the arena (can't blast your opponent's face — this isn't Blower Duel). It swings left-right and tilts forward-back, giving ~80% of the table coverage from each end.

**Player grip:** The nozzle end has a pistol-grip handle with a squeeze trigger (inline valve). Squeeze = air flows. Release = stops. The player holds the nozzle like a sci-fi weapon, sweeping it across the town and squeezing to blast.

The boom arm serves triple duty:
1. **Safety** — nozzle always points down into the arena, never at faces
2. **Ergonomics** — arm supports the hose weight, player only steers the lightweight nozzle
3. **Drama** — the boom arm looks like a crane or mechanical arm. It *looks* like you're controlling a storm from above

### Airflow Tuning

The inline damper valve on each hose lets the operator tune wind power before the event:
- **Too strong:** Ping pong balls fly off the table, debris goes everywhere, no control
- **Too weak:** Balls barely move, no spectacle
- **Sweet spot:** Balls roll and skitter across the table at ~2-3 ft/sec. Debris lifts and swirls but mostly stays in the arena. Streamers snap horizontal. Pinwheels spin fast. Player can "push" a ball across 3 feet of open road in ~1.5 seconds, but buildings block and redirect the wind, requiring repositioning

The damper valve is the critical tuning knob that v1 didn't have. V1's bucket cannons had exactly one power setting (however hard you pulled). V2 lets you dial in the exact experience.

## Are There Really Little Tornados?

**Not literal vortices, but the visual effect is close.**

When two blowers fire from opposite ends simultaneously, the crossing airstreams create turbulent zones in the center of the table. Debris caught in the crossfire genuinely swirls — not in a tight funnel, but in chaotic circular patterns that *look* like miniature storm systems. Ribbon streamers in the center zone flutter in rapidly changing directions. Ping pong balls caught in the crossfire wobble and change direction unpredictably.

With the optional fog machine (see below), these turbulent zones become visible as swirling fog — the closest you'll get to a real tabletop tornado without a vortex generator.

The effect is strongest in the **dead zone** — the center third of the table where neither player has clean wind lines. Balls that enter the dead zone become contested: both players blasting, streamers whipping, debris swirling, ball wobbling between two competing gusts. That's the drama.

### Tier 1: Built-In (No Extra Cost)

- **Mylar pinwheels** on fence posts — 12 total, flash and spin
- **Confetti/debris field** — scattered before each round, creates visual chaos
- **Streamer "wind socks"** at each corner — longer ribbons (12") that show overall wind direction

### Tier 2: Fog Machine ($25 upgrade)
- Small 400W fog machine positioned at the midpoint of one long side
- Low-lying fog fills the arena to ~3" depth at the start of each round
- As players blast, fog MOVES — you can see the airflow patterns in real time
- Crossing air streams create visible turbulent swirls in the center
- Fog dissipates over the 60-second round, creating a natural visual arc (foggy chaos → clear precision)
- **This is the single biggest spectacle upgrade.** Fog makes invisible air visible. Every blast carves a visible path through the mist. The first 10 seconds of a round, when the fog is thickest, look genuinely cinematic

### Tier 3: UV Blacklight ($15 upgrade)
- Two 24" UV LED bar lights mounted on the boom arm posts, aimed down at the table
- Fluorescent ping pong balls (neon green/orange) GLOW under UV

- Surveyor's flagging tape (often fluorescent pink/orange) glows intensely
- **Night mode:** At dusk or in a shaded booth, the UV + fog combo creates a glowing storm table that's visible from across the field. People walk over just to see what's happening

## Gameplay

### Setup (Operator)
1. Place buildings on dowel mounts (pre-marked positions, 30 seconds)
2. Scatter 6 ping pong balls each (red + blue, 12 total) randomly across Main Street
3. Scatter a handful of confetti/debris
4. Optional: trigger fog machine for 5-second burst
5. Hand nozzles to players

### Play (60 Seconds)
1. **BLAST** — Squeeze the trigger. Wind pours from your nozzle down into the arena
2. **STEER** — Sweep the boom arm left/right, tilt forward/back. Push YOUR color balls toward your storm shelters
3. **BLOCK** — Buildings redirect your wind. The church steeple splits your airstream. The water tower creates a dead zone behind it. You have to angle around obstacles
4. **CONTEST** — Balls in the center dead zone get pushed by both players. Streamers are going crazy. Debris is swirling. The ball wobbles back and forth until someone finds the angle
5. **SCORE** — Ball drops into a shelter. Flap CLACKS shut. Flag pops up. That ball is locked in — no blowing it back out
6. **SABOTAGE** — Nothing stops you from blowing your OPPONENT'S balls away from their shelters. Every second on offense is a second you're not scoring. The choice is real

### Scoring
| Shelter | Position | Opening | Points |
|---|---|---|---|
| Town Hall (center) | Dead center of table | 2.5" — barely wider than the ball | 100 |
| Church Cellar | Mid-field, offset left | 3" | 50 |
| School Basement ×2 | Mid-flanks | 3.5" | 25 |
| Barn Storm Shelter ×2 | Near player ends | 4" | 10 |

Note: Each player has one Barn shelter near their own end (easy, low value) and shares all center/mid shelters. The 100-point Town Hall is equidistant from both players and in the dead zone — the hardest, most contested score.

### End
Timer buzzes (tornado siren sound effect from a Bluetooth speaker). Count shelter scores. Highest total wins.

## The Buildings as Gameplay

Buildings aren't decoration — they're the peg field equivalent. They redirect wind, create dead zones, and force players to think about angles.

| Building | Wind Effect |
|---|---|
| Water Tower (cylinder) | Wind splits around it. Creates a calm pocket directly behind it where balls collect and are hard to move |
| Church + Steeple (tall, pointed) | Steeple deflects downward-angled wind upward — balls near the church get "sheltered" from overhead blasts |
| General Store (wide rectangle) | Creates the largest wind shadow. A ball behind the store requires the player to reposition 90° to reach it |
| Houses (small cubes) | Minor deflection. Create channels between them that accelerate wind (Venturi effect) — balls in channels move faster |
| Fences (low walls with gaps) | Block ground-level rolling balls but let air pass over the top. Player has to angle their blast downward to push balls under/through fence gaps |
| Trees (cylinders on bases) | Minimal blockage but create unpredictable deflection. Balls that clip a tree spin off in random directions |

The layout is designed so there's NO clear straight-line path from either player's end to the center shelters. Every scoring attempt requires navigating wind around at least 2-3 buildings. That's where the skill lives.

## What Kids Remember

1. **"I controlled a tornado"** — The boom arm + nozzle + visible wind effects create a genuine feeling of controlling a storm. Not pushing buttons. Not throwing balls. Controlling wind
2. **"The fog was swirling"** — If using the fog machine, the visible airflow is the single most memorable visual. Kids have never seen a tabletop where you can watch wind move
3. **"Everything was flying around"** — Ribbons, pinwheels, confetti, balls — the table is constantly in motion. It looks chaotic even from a distance
4. **"I blew his ball away from the shelter right before it went in"** — The sabotage moment. Your ball is 2 inches from the shelter opening, your opponent sweeps their nozzle and blasts it sideways. That moment creates a story
