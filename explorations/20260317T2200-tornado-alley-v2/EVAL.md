# Tornado Alley v2 — Carnival Game Evaluation

## Scoring Rubric
Each heuristic scored 1–10 (10 = best possible).

---

### Overall Entertainment: 8/10
The continuous blower mechanic solves v1's fatal flaw — no reload downtime, no waiting. Squeeze the trigger and wind flows. The boom arm sweep gives directional control that feels physical and intentional. Pushing a ping pong ball across a table with wind through a maze of buildings has a satisfying indirect-control feel — you're influencing, not placing. The sabotage option (blowing opponent's balls away from their shelters) adds a real strategic choice every second of the round.

What keeps this from a 9: the player's physical action is just "point and squeeze." There's no impact moment, no THWACK, no ball-in-hand. The satisfaction is visual and strategic, not kinesthetic. Compare to Flipper Frenzy where you're constantly squeezing flippers with two hands, reacting to multiple cascading balls. Tornado Alley is more cerebral, less frantic. That's fine for 4th-5th graders but may feel slow to a 2nd grader who wants to DO something with their hands.

### Memorability: 8/10
The visible wind effects carry this score. Forty ribbon streamers snapping in different directions, pinwheels spinning up, confetti lifting off the table — this creates a visual that doesn't exist at any other carnival booth. With the fog upgrade, the airflow becomes literally visible, which is genuinely novel. "I controlled a tornado and the fog moved" is a sentence a kid actually says.

Without the fog, memorability drops to a 7. Ribbon streamers are fun but kids don't tell stories about ribbons. The fog is what makes the invisible visible, and the visible is what makes memories. The base concept (blowing balls across a table with a hose) is dangerously close to "I played with a leaf blower" without the spectacle upgrades.

### Ease of Construction: 6/10
The table is straightforward plywood — any parent volunteer with a drill can build it. Buildings are scrap wood blocks and soup cans, painted. That's the easy 60%.

The hard 40%: Two PVC boom arms that need to pivot smoothly under variable loads (hose weight + player pulling), with counterbalances dialed in so the nozzle doesn't droop or fly up. The nozzle cone reducers are 3D-printed (printer dependency, though only 2 parts). The inline damper valves need real-world tuning — too much wind and balls fly off the table, too little and nothing moves. The shelter flag pop-up levers are fiddly mechanisms that will eat hours of prototyping time for a payoff that's mostly cosmetic (you could just peek in the shelter to count balls).

Compared to v1 (score: 5), this is meaningfully simpler — no 6 vortex cannons to build and tune, no acrylic lid to cut and mount, no bucket membranes to tension. But it's still a multi-system build (table + boom arms + blowers + shelters + wind decorations) that takes a dedicated weekend.

### Skill to Luck Spectrum: 7/10 (Skill-leaning, with real decisions)
**Skill factors:** Boom arm positioning is precise — you choose which ball to target, what angle to approach from, whether to go around the left or right side of a building. Reading the streamers tells you where your wind is actually going. The sabotage choice (score vs. disrupt) is a genuine strategic decision every second.

**Luck factors:** Ping pong balls on a flat surface are twitchy — small imperfections in the tabletop, turbulence from the opponent's crosswind, and ball-on-ball collisions create unpredictability. The contested dead zone in the center is deliberately chaotic. A ball aimed at the 100-point Town Hall may get caught in the opponent's crosswind and end up in a 10-point Barn.

The skill-luck balance is good for a carnival game. A skilled player wins most of the time, but a lucky blast can score 100 points and close a gap. The strategic layer (offense vs. defense, target selection, building pathing) gives it more depth than most booths.

### Chaos Level: 7/10
With both blowers firing, the table is visually busy — ribbons fluttering, pinwheels spinning, debris scattering, balls rolling in multiple directions. The dead zone in the center creates a visible crosswind conflict. That's genuine chaos.

But it's not Flipper Frenzy-level pandemonium (9/10). There are 12 balls on the table but they roll at ~2-3 ft/sec, not cascading at gravity speed. The action is continuous but not frantic — you're sweeping and aiming, not reacting to six simultaneous emergencies. If a kid wants "barely contained riot," this isn't it. If they want "I'm a storm chaser and everything is blowing around," it delivers.

Fog bumps this to an 8 — visible airflow patterns add a layer of visual chaos that ribbon streamers alone can't match.

### Durability: 7/10
**Strong points:** Plywood table is sturdy. PVC boom arms are cheap and replaceable. Ping pong balls are $4 for a 12-pack. Buildings are solid wood blocks. Blowers are commercial products rated for hours of continuous use. Ribbon streamers are literally surveyor's tape — $3 for 300 feet.

**Weak points:** Ping pong balls will crack — they're getting blown into wood blocks and table walls all day. Budget 2-3 extra packs. The boom arm PVC pivot joints will develop slop after hundreds of rotations — Teflon tape or bushings help but won't eliminate it. The squeeze inline valve is a light-duty part being operated by kids all day — it may leak or stiffen. The foam/tissue debris will get soggy in humidity and needs to be replenished throughout the day.

**Repairability:** Everything that wears out is cheap and standardized. No custom parts except the 3D-printed nozzle reducers (print 2 spares). The boom arm can be disassembled and rebuilt from PVC in 20 minutes. The single most likely failure (cracked ping pong balls) is a $4 fix.

### Photogenicity: 8/10
This is the concept's stealth weapon. From a parent's phone camera:
- Kids gripping nozzles on mechanical arms = looks cool and unusual
- Table full of fluttering ribbons and spinning pinwheels = visible motion even in a still photo
- Scattered confetti and debris = "action shot" texture
- With fog: visible wind paths carved through mist = genuinely photogenic and unique
- With UV: glowing balls and fluorescent ribbons under blacklight = unmistakably eye-catching from distance

The open-top design means no glare (v1's acrylic lid problem is gone). Parents can shoot straight down into the arena or from the side. The boom arms give the booth a distinctive silhouette — you can identify it from across the field.

Without upgrades, this is a 7 (ribbons and pinwheels are pretty but small). With fog + UV, it's a legitimate 9 — there's nothing else at a school carnival that looks like a glowing fog table with wind tunnels.

### Onboarding Time: 8/10 (Near-instant concept, minimal physical learning curve)
**15-second explanation:** "Squeeze the trigger to blow air. Push YOUR color balls into the shelters. You can blow your opponent's balls away too. 60 seconds. Go."

The boom arm has intuitive ergonomics — grab the handle, point it at the table, squeeze. No complex mechanics to learn. No reload. No technique. The streamers provide instant feedback ("my wind is going THAT way"). A kid who has never seen the game understands the core loop within 5 seconds of their first squeeze.

The only onboarding friction: understanding that you CAN sabotage (many kids will just focus on their own balls). A small sign saying "BLOW YOUR OPPONENT'S BALLS AWAY!" solves this.

## Verdict

Tornado Alley v2 is a genuine concept — not a gimmick search. It fixes every mechanical failure that killed v1 (unreliable cannons → proven blowers, invisible air → visible wind effects, reload downtime → continuous action, acrylic lid → open top) while keeping the theme that made v1 worth exploring in the first place.

The core mechanic (blowing ping pong balls across a tabletop with a boom-arm-mounted nozzle) is testable in 30 minutes with a bounce house inflator and some ping pong balls on a table. If the balls move controllably at 3 feet, the concept works. Unlike v1's vortex cannons, there is zero uncertainty about whether a continuous-flow blower can push a 2.7g ball across a flat surface. It can. The only tuning question is the damper setting, and that's adjustable.

**What it sacrifices:** Kinesthetic intensity. This is a "point and steer" game, not a "grab and slam" game. Players who want to feel impact in their hands (Flipper Frenzy's dual-flipper squeeze, Blower Duel's face-blast drama) will find this contemplative by comparison. The fun is in reading the wind, outsmarting your opponent, and watching the visual spectacle — not in the physical act of squeezing a trigger.

**When NOT to build it:** If your carnival audience skews young (mostly K-1st), the indirect air mechanic will confuse and frustrate. Build Flipper Frenzy instead. If you can't get electrical power to your booth (two blowers need outlets), this is dead on arrival. If you're indoors, skip the fog machine or you'll set off the fire alarm.

**When to build it:** If you want a booth that looks spectacular from 30 feet away, photographs beautifully, and creates a unique experience no kid has had before. The fog + UV upgrade turns this from "good carnival game" into "the booth everyone walks over to see." At $360-400 fully loaded, it's competitively priced with concepts that deliver less visual spectacle.

**Recommendation:** Build it — but commit to the fog upgrade. Without fog, this is a 7/10 concept with nice ribbons. With fog, it's an 8.5/10 concept with a visual signature that no other booth can match. The $25 fog machine is the highest-ROI single purchase in this entire repo.
