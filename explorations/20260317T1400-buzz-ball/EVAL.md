# BUZZ BALL — Carnival Game Evaluation

## Scoring (1–10 scale)

### Overall Entertainment: 9/10
Spring plunger launches give satisfying tactile feedback. Balls cascading through hex pegs is visually mesmerizing. But the real star: the Bee Bumpers. A volunteer behind the board cranking chaos into the game in real time creates unpredictable, laugh-out-loud moments. The brass bell ringing for a Queen's Cell hit is a crowd event. The only thing keeping it from 10 is the slight downtime between alternating player turns (vs. simultaneous play).

### Memorability: 10/10
This is the game kids talk about for years. The honeycomb aesthetic is unique — nobody has seen a hex-peg plinko board before. The Beekeeper volunteer becomes a character. The bee bumpers are a "wait, WHAT just happened?" mechanic that creates stories ("my ball was about to hit 200 and the bumper sent it to 50!"). It's Grattan-branded — it's THEIR game. The brass bell for the Queen's Cell creates highlight moments. This will be photographed, filmed, and remembered.

### Ease of Construction: 6/10
The base plinko board is straightforward lumber and plywood. The hex peg caps require a 3D printer (90 caps is a big batch but simple geometry). The scoring buckets are standard woodworking. **The bee bumper mechanism is the challenge** — cam axle, compression springs, pushrod alignment, and bushing installation require moderate mechanical skill. Not impossible for two handy dads, but it's the most complex subsystem. The cam timing (120° offset) needs careful machining or 3D printing. Budget a full weekend day just for bumper assembly and tuning. Overall: doable with Milwaukee tools and patience, but this is a step up from basic carnival game construction.

### Skill to Luck Spectrum: 8/10 (excellent balance)
- **Skill component:** Pull strength on the plunger controls launch height. Gentle pull = safer low-zone pegs, easy 50/100 pts. Full pull = high-zone with more chaos, access to 200/500 but also more bumper exposure. Learning curve is fast and rewarding
- **Luck component:** Hex peg bounces are inherently chaotic (6-directional vs. 2-directional on square grids). Bee bumpers add massive randomness — you can't predict when the volunteer cranks. Ball paths through the field are genuinely unpredictable
- **The balance:** A skilled player consistently scores higher by choosing smart pull strengths, but anyone can get lucky with a bumper-assisted Queen's Cell hit. This means the 2nd grader CAN beat the 5th grader, and that moment is magic

### Chaos Level: 9/10
Hex geometry creates 3× more bounce vectors than traditional plinko. Three bee bumpers popping in sequence can send multiple balls flying simultaneously. The volunteer controls the chaos throttle — fast cranking = pure pandemonium, slow cranking = dramatic tension. The gap between "my ball is heading for 200 points" and "a bumper just launched it into the POLLEN bucket" is zero seconds. Only docked 1 point because the alternating-turns format means chaos affects one ball at a time rather than a simultaneous multi-ball frenzy.

### Durability: 8/10
- **Board & frame:** Plywood + lumber + carriage bolts. Basically indestructible. Will last years with minimal touch-up paint
- **Hex peg caps:** 3D printed PLA. Cheap to reprint if any crack. Friction-fit means easy replacement
- **Acrylic front:** Shatter-resistant ¼". Would need replacement only from extreme impact
- **Bee bumpers:** The main wear point. Springs may fatigue after heavy use. Cam surfaces wear against pushrods. **Mitigation:** springs are $3 each to replace, cam lobes are quick 3D prints. Budget 30 min pre-carnival maintenance
- **Plunger assemblies:** Standard pinball parts, designed for thousands of cycles
- **Brass bell:** Literally a bell. Will outlive us all

### Photogenicity: 9/10
Gold-and-black honeycomb on a 6-foot board is a BACKDROP. Clear acrylic front means you can photograph balls mid-cascade. The Beekeeper volunteer in a hat and veil is inherently photogenic. Yellow pollen balls against the dark honeycomb are high-contrast and pop in photos. The hex geometry reads as intentional and designed, not DIY-janky. Parents will photograph their kids playing, other parents will photograph the board itself. Only dock a point because the side/back view (where the mechanism lives) is less pretty — but that's behind the scenes.

### Onboarding Time: 9/10 (very fast — closer to cornhole)
- **Explain in one sentence:** "Pull the plunger, launch the ball, highest score wins"
- **Rules kids need to know:** Pull harder = ball goes higher = more risk and reward. 5 balls each, take turns
- **What they don't need to know (but discover):** The bumpers exist. The first time a bumper pops and sends a ball flying, the kid's face is the content. No explanation needed — the mechanic is self-evident
- **Zero learning curve on the plunger** — pull and release, same as every spring toy they've ever used
- **Signage:** Point values are on the buckets. That's it. Kids will self-organize

## Verdict
Buzz Ball is the game that defines the Grattan carnival. The bee bumper mechanic is unlike anything at any school carnival anywhere — it's a genuine innovation that creates stories, drama, and screaming. The honeycomb aesthetic makes it unmistakably Grattan's. The construction is the hardest part (that cam mechanism), but the payoff is a game that will run for years with minimal maintenance. Two dads with Milwaukee tools can build this in 3 weekends. The kids will remember it for a decade.
